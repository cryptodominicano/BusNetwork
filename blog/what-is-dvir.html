What Is DVIR? The Complete Guide for Bus Fleets 2026

By BusFleetAI | March 31, 2026 | 5 min read

DVIR stands for Driver Vehicle Inspection Report. It is a federal DOT requirement under 49 CFR 396.11 and 396.13 mandating that every commercial bus driver complete a written vehicle inspection before and after every trip. Missing or incomplete DVIRs expose bus fleets to fines, failed audits, and increased CSA scores — and most violations are entirely preventable with automated compliance monitoring.

What Does DVIR Stand For?

DVIR stands for Driver Vehicle Inspection Report. The FMCSA — Federal Motor Carrier Safety Administration — requires every driver of a commercial motor vehicle to inspect their vehicle before and after every trip and record the results in a written report. The purpose is to catch safety defects before they cause accidents, breakdowns, or regulatory violations on the road.

Which Buses Are Required to Complete DVIRs?

Any commercial motor vehicle operating in interstate commerce is subject to DVIR requirements. This includes charter buses, private motorcoaches, shuttle buses, and school buses operated by private contractors. Buses operating exclusively within a single state may be subject to state-level equivalents. When in doubt, treat every commercial bus as DVIR-required.

What Does a DVIR Inspection Cover?

Federal regulations specify the minimum items a driver must inspect. These include service brakes and parking brake, steering mechanism, all lighting including headlights and brake lights, tires and wheels, horn, windshield wipers and washers, mirrors, emergency equipment including fire extinguisher and reflectors, and coupling devices if applicable. The driver must note any defects and sign the report whether defects are found or not.

What Happens When a Bus Fleet Misses a DVIR?

Missing DVIRs are a federal violation with real consequences. During a DOT compliance review, each missing or incomplete report can result in a per-violation fine. A pattern of missing reports raises a fleet's CSA score — Compliance, Safety, Accountability — which can trigger more frequent inspections, higher insurance premiums, and in serious cases, out-of-service orders that ground vehicles until compliance is restored. The fines themselves are often less costly than the downstream effects.
